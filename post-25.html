How Social Media Has Changed What Clients Ask For

For Better and Worse

Pending Review
SEO Meta Description

Pinterest boards and Instagram saves have transformed how clients communicate their vision. MDM Design Studio shares an honest take on how social media shapes the custom furniture process — the good and the complicated.

When a client walks into a design consultation today, they usually come prepared in a way that clients ten or fifteen years ago rarely did. They've been saving images for months. They have a Pinterest board called "Dream Kitchen" with 400 pins. They know what cerused oak looks like. They know the difference between a Shaker door and a flat-panel door. They have an opinion about hardware.

This is mostly wonderful. But it comes with complications that are worth being clear-eyed about.

The Good: Better Communication, Faster Alignment

The most significant impact social media has had on our design conversations is the quality of initial communication. When a client can show us exactly the aesthetic they're drawn to — not just in words but in images — we align on the design direction faster and with less guesswork.

Platform literacy has also raised the overall level of design ambition we see in clients. People who have been steeped in well-designed spaces through Instagram and Houzz develop a visual sensibility that makes our conversations richer and our outcomes better.

The Complicated: When the Image Isn't the Brief

Social media images are curated, staged, filtered, and often represent spaces that don't function as real homes. The kitchen photograph that became the reference image for a client's renovation may have been built for a photo shoot and never cooked in.

When clients bring inspiration images, we always look at two things: what do they love about this image, and is that thing achievable in their actual context?

The Trend Cycle Problem

Social media also accelerates trend cycles in ways that create real design risk. A finish, a form, or a material can go from unknown to ubiquitous to oversaturated in twelve to eighteen months on social media.

We take this seriously, because our job isn't to validate whatever is currently dominating the algorithm. It's to help clients make decisions they'll love long-term. We'll always note when a client's inspiration is leaning heavily on something that reads as trend-forward, and we'll have an honest conversation about how to capture the spirit of it in a way that has more longevity.

The Renderings and Reality Gap

One of the side effects of polished design visualization software is that clients sometimes arrive with expectations calibrated against computer-generated images rather than real spaces. Real furniture, in a real room, with real light, is better in the ways that matter — tactile, alive, genuinely present.

We work to set realistic expectations throughout the process and use our portfolio of real, photographed work as the primary reference for what our output actually looks like.

The way clients come to us today has changed our conversations in genuinely exciting ways. We love a well-prepared client with a clear visual brief. We just also love being the people who help them think past the algorithm. Get in touch and bring your inspiration boards — we're ready for them.
